Module 8: The Challenge

A Real EEG. A Real Question. Your Diagnosis.

A 68-year-old retired teacher is brought to the emergency department by her daughter. Over the past two days, the patient has had episodes of “staring and not responding” lasting 30–60 seconds, sometimes with subtle lip-smacking. Between episodes she seems confused and drowsy. The ED attending orders a stat EEG. The technician places 19 electrodes using the standard 10-20 system and begins recording. Twenty minutes later, the neurologist pulls up the recording and turns to you: “Take a look at this. Tell me what you see.”

Everything you’ve learned in Modules 1–7 comes together here. This is the graded capstone: you will load a real EEG recording, examine it systematically, and write a brief clinical interpretation — exactly what a neurology resident does every day.

What You’ll Need

  • Python (3.8+) with the mne and numpy libraries, OR MATLAB with basic signal processing
  • About 90 minutes of focused time
  • This tutorial site open in another tab for reference

If you don’t have mne installed:

pip install mne numpy matplotlib

The Data

We’ll use a publicly available EEG recording from the Temple University Hospital (TUH) EEG Corpus — the largest public clinical EEG dataset in the world. Your instructor will provide the specific .edf file or a download link.

If you’re working with a simulated dataset provided by your instructor, the same analysis steps apply.

EDF (European Data Format) is the standard file format for clinical EEG. It stores multi-channel time series data along with metadata: patient info, electrode labels, sampling rate, and calibration. The mne library reads it directly.

Loading the Data

import mne
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Load the EDF file (replace with your actual file path)
raw = mne.io.read_raw_edf("patient_eeg.edf", preload=True)

# Basic info
print(raw.info)
print(f"Channels: {raw.ch_names}")
print(f"Sampling rate: {raw.info['sfreq']} Hz")
print(f"Duration: {raw.n_times / raw.info['sfreq']:.1f} seconds")

# Plot the raw data (scroll through it)
raw.plot(duration=10, n_channels=19, scalings='auto', title='Raw EEG')
plt.show()

Take a moment to scroll through the recording. Get a feel for it. Don’t try to interpret yet — just look.

Your Tasks

There are five tasks. Each builds on the previous one.

Task 1: Orientation (10 minutes)

Before you analyze anything, answer these basic questions about the recording:

  1. How many channels are in the recording?
  2. What is the sampling rate?
  3. How long is the recording (in seconds)?
  4. What montage is being used? (Check the channel names — are they labeled as individual electrodes like “Fp1”, or as differences like “Fp1-F3”?)
  5. What is the amplitude range of the signals in microvolts?

Write your answers in your report. These are the first things a neurologist checks when opening any EEG — it’s how you orient yourself to the data.

Task 2: Artifact Identification (20 minutes)

Scroll through the recording and identify at least three distinct artifacts. For each artifact:

  1. What type of artifact is it? (Refer to Module 6: blink, eye movement, muscle, ECG, electrode pop, 60 Hz line noise, movement)
  2. Which channels are affected?
  3. When does it occur? (Give approximate time stamps)
  4. How do you know it’s an artifact and not brain activity? (One sentence is enough)

Tip: Plot a short segment (2–5 seconds) around each artifact. Annotate or screenshot it for your report. Use filtering to confirm your identification — for example, a notch filter at 60 Hz should eliminate line noise but not brain activity.

# Example: zoom in on a 5-second segment starting at t=30s
start, stop = int(30 * raw.info['sfreq']), int(35 * raw.info['sfreq'])
data, times = raw[:, start:stop]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))
for i, ch_name in enumerate(raw.ch_names[:10]):
    ax.plot(times, data[i] * 1e6 + i * 100, label=ch_name)
ax.set_xlabel('Time (s)')
ax.set_ylabel('Channel (offset for display)')
ax.set_title('EEG Segment: 30-35 seconds')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Task 3: Frequency Analysis (20 minutes)

Pick a 10-second segment of clean EEG (minimal artifacts). Then:

  1. Compute the power spectrum (FFT) of at least two channels.
  2. Identify the dominant frequency in each channel. Which EEG band does it fall in? (Delta: 0.5–4 Hz, Theta: 4–8 Hz, Alpha: 8–13 Hz, Beta: 13–30 Hz)
  3. Compute and display a spectrogram for one channel over a longer segment (at least 60 seconds). Describe what you see — is the frequency content stable, or does it change over time?
# Power spectrum of a clean segment
clean_start, clean_stop = 60, 70  # seconds (adjust to a clean segment you found)
clean_data = raw.copy().crop(tmin=clean_start, tmax=clean_stop)

# Compute PSD using Welch's method
spectrum = clean_data.compute_psd(method='welch', fmin=0.5, fmax=40)
spectrum.plot(average=True)
plt.title('Power Spectral Density — Clean Segment')
plt.show()

# Spectrogram of a single channel
from matplotlib.mlab import specgram

ch_idx = raw.ch_names.index('C3')  # pick a central channel
long_data, long_times = raw[ch_idx, :]
long_data = long_data.flatten() * 1e6  # convert to microvolts

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 4))
Pxx, freqs, bins, im = ax.specgram(
    long_data, NFFT=int(raw.info['sfreq'] * 2),
    Fs=raw.info['sfreq'], noverlap=int(raw.info['sfreq']),
    cmap='viridis', vmin=-20, vmax=30
)
ax.set_ylim(0, 30)
ax.set_xlabel('Time (s)')
ax.set_ylabel('Frequency (Hz)')
ax.set_title('Spectrogram — Channel C3')
plt.colorbar(im, ax=ax, label='Power (dB)')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

What to look for in the spectrogram: Is there a persistent alpha band? Does the frequency content shift suddenly at any point? A sudden change in the spectrogram — especially an evolving pattern of rhythmic activity — could indicate a seizure or other clinically significant event. Compare what you see to the seizure spectrogram description in Module 7.

Task 4: Find the Abnormality (20 minutes)

This is the clinical core. Something is abnormal in this recording. It might be:

  • Epileptiform discharges (spikes, sharp waves, spike-and-wave complexes)
  • Focal slowing (one region showing slower frequencies than the rest)
  • Generalized slowing (all channels showing abnormally slow activity)
  • A seizure (evolving rhythmic activity)
  • Asymmetry (one hemisphere looks different from the other)

Your job:

  1. Describe the abnormality. What does it look like in the raw EEG? (Shape, duration, amplitude, repetition rate)
  2. Localize it. Which channels show it most prominently? What brain region does that correspond to?
  3. Time it. When does it start? Does it evolve? How long does it last?
  4. Show your evidence. Include at least two plots:
    • A raw EEG segment showing the abnormality
    • A spectrogram or power spectrum that highlights it

Strategy: Don’t stare at every second of the recording looking for something weird. Use the spectrogram as your map — it compresses the entire recording into one image. Look for sudden changes in the frequency pattern. Then go back to the raw EEG at those time points and examine what’s happening.

Task 5: Clinical Interpretation (20 minutes)

Write a brief clinical EEG report as if you were dictating it for the medical record. Use this structure:

Clinical History: [1–2 sentences summarizing the patient and the reason for the EEG]

Technical Description: [Recording parameters — channels, sampling rate, duration, montage]

Background Activity: [What does the normal background look like? Is it symmetric? What is the dominant posterior rhythm?]

Abnormal Findings: [Describe each abnormality you found — type, location, morphology, frequency]

Artifacts: [List the artifacts you identified and how they were handled]

Clinical Correlation: [What do these findings suggest? What clinical condition is most consistent with this EEG? What would you recommend as a next step?]

Your report doesn’t need to be long. A real clinical EEG report is usually half a page. Precision and clarity matter more than length.

Submission

Submit a single document (PDF preferred) containing:

  1. Answers to all five tasks with the requested plots and descriptions
  2. Your Python/MATLAB code (as an appendix or in code blocks within the document)
  3. Your clinical EEG report (Task 5)

Label each section clearly. Include figure captions.

Grading criteria:

  • Completeness — Did you address all five tasks?
  • Accuracy — Are your artifact identifications correct? Did you find the real abnormality?
  • Clinical reasoning — Does your interpretation make sense given the findings?
  • Code quality — Does your code run? Is it readable?
  • Communication — Is your report clear, concise, and professional?

A Final Word

This is the hardest module in the tutorial, and it’s meant to be. Everything before this was guided — sliders, widgets, immediate feedback. Here, you’re on your own with real data and a real question.

That’s what clinical medicine is. The patient doesn’t come with a quiz answer key. You have to look at the data, apply what you know, and make a judgment.

You have all the tools. Go use them.
