Module 2: Sampling

Capturing the Signal — From Continuous Body to Discrete Computer

A technician is setting up an EEG for a patient with suspected temporal lobe epilepsy. The attending neurologist glances at the machine settings and asks: “What sampling rate are you using?” The tech replies: “256 Hz.” The attending nods and moves on. But why 256? Why not 100? Why not 10,000? And what happens if you get it wrong?

You now know what signals look like — smooth, continuous waves with amplitude and frequency. But here’s the problem: your body produces continuous signals (voltage that changes every infinitesimal instant), and computers only understand discrete numbers (a list of measurements at specific times). To get that EEG onto a screen, we need to sample it — measure the voltage at regular intervals.

How often is often enough? That’s the central question of this module. Get it right, and you faithfully capture the brain’s activity. Get it wrong, and the signal you see on screen is a lie.

From Continuous to Discrete

Imagine someone singing a smooth, continuous note. Now imagine you can only listen for a brief instant once per second. If the note changes slowly, you’d follow along fine. But if the note is wobbling rapidly — faster than you’re checking — you’d miss what’s happening. Worse, you might think you’re hearing something that isn’t there.

That’s sampling. The rate at which you “check” the signal is called the sampling rate (or sampling frequency, \(f_s\)), measured in Hz — samples per second.

The Nyquist Rule

Harry Nyquist figured this out in the 1920s: to faithfully capture a signal of frequency \(f\), you must sample at a rate of at least \(2f\) — twice the highest frequency present.

\[f_s \geq 2 \times f_{max}\]

Below that threshold, the signal aliases — it masquerades as a lower frequency. The math is surprisingly clean: a signal at frequency \(f\) sampled at rate \(f_s\) will appear at the aliased frequency \(|f - n \cdot f_s|\), where \(n\) is the nearest integer.

The widget below makes this visceral. A fixed 10 Hz signal, and you control the sampling rate.


The Nyquist moment:

  • Start at 50 Hz. The samples clearly trace the 10 Hz wave.
  • Slowly lower the sampling rate toward 20 Hz. It still works, but barely.
  • Drop below 20 Hz. A dashed red alias wave appears — the computer now “sees” a completely different frequency.
  • Try exactly 20 Hz (the Nyquist limit). Notice the samples land on zero crossings — you get zero amplitude. The Nyquist rate is the theoretical minimum; in practice, you need more than \(2f\).

The Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem: To capture a signal containing frequencies up to \(f_{max}\) Hz, you must sample at \(f_s > 2 f_{max}\). Below this rate, high-frequency content folds back as lower-frequency aliases — phantom signals that don’t actually exist in the patient’s body. Clinical systems typically sample at 4–5 times the maximum frequency of interest as a safety margin.

Clinical Sampling Rates

In practice, nobody samples at exactly \(2f_{max}\) — that’s the bare minimum, and any imperfection causes aliasing. Real systems add a generous safety margin. Here’s what you’ll see in clinical settings:

Signal Type Frequencies of Interest Minimum Nyquist Rate Typical Clinical Rate
EEG 0.5–100 Hz 200 Hz 256–512 Hz
ECG (Holter) 0.5–100 Hz 200 Hz 125–250 Hz
ECG (diagnostic) 0.5–150 Hz 300 Hz 500–1000 Hz
EMG 20–500 Hz 1000 Hz 1000–5000 Hz

Why 256 Hz for EEG? Because the highest frequency neurologists typically care about is around 100 Hz (the upper end of gamma activity). \(2 \times 100 = 200\) Hz minimum. 256 gives a comfortable margin and happens to be a power of 2 (which makes the computer math faster — we’ll see why in the FFT module).

Why this matters clinically: If a technician accidentally sets the EEG sampling rate too low — say 100 Hz when trying to capture high gamma activity up to 80 Hz — the system is right at the Nyquist edge. Any frequency above 50 Hz will alias down, potentially creating false patterns in the recording that look like real brain activity but aren’t. Aliasing doesn’t announce itself. The signal looks perfectly normal — just wrong. That’s what makes it dangerous.

What About Anti-Aliasing?

One more thing. In real clinical systems, there’s a hardware filter before the analog-to-digital converter called an anti-aliasing filter. It’s a low-pass filter (we’ll learn about those in the next module) that removes any frequency above \(f_s/2\) before sampling. This guarantees nothing above the Nyquist frequency makes it into the digitized signal.

You don’t need to build one — the equipment manufacturer does. But you should know it exists, because it means the sampling rate you choose determines the highest frequency you can ever see in the recording. Set \(f_s\) = 256 Hz, and frequencies above 128 Hz are gone forever — the anti-aliasing filter removed them before they were recorded.

Summary

We captured the signal digitally. The key ideas:

  1. Sampling converts a continuous signal into discrete numbers at regular intervals.
  2. The Nyquist theorem says: sample at more than \(2 \times f_{max}\) or you’ll get aliasing.
  3. Aliasing creates phantom frequencies in your recording — signals that look real but aren’t.
  4. Clinical systems use generous safety margins (4–5 times the maximum frequency of interest).
  5. Anti-aliasing filters in the hardware prevent aliasing before it happens.

The signal is now digital. But look at it — there’s noise everywhere. Power line hum at 60 Hz, slow electrode drift, muscle artifacts. How do we clean it up without throwing away the brain activity? That’s filtering.
