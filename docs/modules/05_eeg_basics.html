Module 5: EEG Basics

Reading the Brain

You’re a junior doctor on your neurology rotation. The EEG tech rolls in the machine and starts placing electrodes on a patient’s scalp. There are 21 of them, in very specific positions. The patient looks nervous. “Does this hurt?” she asks. “Not at all,” you say. But you’re thinking: where exactly are those electrodes going, and what will they pick up?

You now have the signal processing toolkit: you know amplitude and frequency, you can sample without aliasing, filter noise, and decompose signals into frequency components. Time to point those tools at the brain.

EEG — electroencephalography — records the electrical activity of the cerebral cortex through electrodes placed on the scalp. The signal you see is the summed postsynaptic potentials of millions of cortical pyramidal neurons, filtered through cerebrospinal fluid, skull, and skin. It’s small (microvolts), it’s noisy, and it carries clinically vital information.

This module covers the three things every doctor needs to know about EEG: where the electrodes go, what the frequency bands mean, and how to recognize basic patterns.

Where the Signal Comes From

Before we talk about electrode placement, it’s worth pausing on what the scalp electrode is actually measuring. The brain’s cortex is full of pyramidal neurons — tall, vertically oriented cells whose apical dendrites reach toward the cortical surface. When thousands of these neurons receive excitatory input at the same time, the tiny postsynaptic voltages along their dendrites sum into a much larger electrical dipole. That dipole is what your scalp electrode picks up.

Two things matter for EEG to exist at all. First, the neurons must be aligned — pyramidal cells are, but interneurons and cerebellar cells are not, which is why those structures don’t show up well on scalp EEG. Second, they must fire synchronously — a single neuron’s PSP is tiny, and by the time it reaches the scalp it’s attenuated thousands of times over. Only summed, synchronous activity survives the trip through CSF, skull, and skin.

The 10-20 System: Where Electrodes Go

The International 10-20 System is the standard electrode placement scheme used worldwide. The “10-20” refers to the fact that electrodes are spaced at 10% or 20% intervals along the skull from standard landmarks (nasion, inion, preauricular points).

Every electrode has a name: a letter indicating the brain region, and a number indicating the hemisphere (odd = left, even = right, z = midline).

  • Click Fp1 or Fp2 (the blue dots at the front). These are closest to the eyes — they pick up eye blink artifacts.
  • Click T3 or T4 (gold dots on the sides). These are critical for detecting temporal lobe seizures — the most common type of epilepsy.

Naming convention: The letter indicates the region (Fp = frontopolar, F = frontal, C = central, T = temporal, P = parietal, O = occipital). Odd numbers = left hemisphere, even numbers = right hemisphere, z = midline. So C3 is left central, C4 is right central, Cz is midline central. This system is universal — it’s the same in a hospital in Tokyo, Toronto, or Tunis.

Alpha Blocking: The First Clinical Test

One of the first things a neurologist checks during an EEG is alpha blocking — whether the alpha rhythm disappears when the patient opens their eyes.

Why? Because the alpha rhythm is generated by the thalamo-cortical loop when the visual cortex is “idling.” When the eyes open and visual input floods the cortex, the idle rhythm is disrupted and alpha drops out, replaced by faster beta activity. If alpha doesn’t block, something may be wrong.

Toggle between eyes-closed and eyes-open conditions below.

Eyes Closed: Strong alpha rhythm (10 Hz) dominates the occipital recording. The patient is relaxed, visual cortex is "idling."

Alpha blocking is one of the most reliable and clinically important EEG phenomena. When a patient closes their eyes, alpha appears over the occipital regions. When they open their eyes, it disappears. If alpha fails to block with eye opening, it raises concern for cortical dysfunction. If it’s absent entirely, consider whether the patient is anxious (beta-dominant), encephalopathic (theta/delta-dominant), or whether it’s a technical issue.

Experiment:

  • Toggle between Eyes Closed and Eyes Open. Watch the alpha peak (10 Hz) shrink dramatically in the spectrum.
  • In the time domain, notice how the eyes-closed trace has a clear ~10 Hz rhythm, while the eyes-open trace looks faster and more “noisy” — that’s beta activity taking over.

Putting It Together: What You See on an EEG Report

When you look at a clinical EEG, the report will describe:

  1. Background activity: Is the dominant posterior rhythm alpha? What’s its frequency and symmetry?
  2. Reactivity: Does it block with eye opening? Does it change with alertness?
  3. Abnormalities: Any focal slowing (theta/delta in one region)? Any epileptiform discharges (spikes, sharp waves)?
  4. Artifacts: Eye blinks, muscle, electrode, 60 Hz noise — all the non-brain stuff (covered in Module 6)

You now have the foundation for understanding all four. You know where the electrodes go, what the frequency bands mean, and what alpha blocking looks like.

Band Range Amplitude Best Location Clinical Significance
Delta 1-4 Hz 100-200 uV Diffuse Normal in deep sleep. Pathological if awake (encephalopathy).
Theta 4-8 Hz 50-100 uV Temporal Normal in drowsiness/children. Focal = possible lesion.
Alpha 8-13 Hz 30-50 uV Occipital Relaxed, eyes closed. Blocks with eye opening.
Beta 13-30 Hz 10-20 uV Frontal/Central Alert state, anxiety, benzodiazepines.
Gamma >30 Hz 5-10 uV Variable Cognitive processing. Often contaminated by muscle artifact.

Summary

You can now read an EEG — or at least, you know the basics:

  1. The 10-20 System places electrodes in standardized positions. Letters = brain region, odd = left, even = right, z = midline.
  2. Five frequency bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma) each carry distinct clinical meaning. Slower = bigger amplitude, faster = smaller.
  3. Alpha blocking (disappearance of the posterior alpha rhythm with eye opening) is a fundamental sign of normal cortical reactivity.

But there’s a catch. Not everything on that EEG screen comes from the brain. Eye blinks, muscle tension, the patient’s heartbeat, a loose electrode, someone’s cell phone — all of these can contaminate the recording.
