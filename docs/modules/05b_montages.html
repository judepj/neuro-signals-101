Module 5b: Montages & Dipoles

Why the Same Brain Looks Different on Different Screens

You’re reviewing an EEG with the attending neurologist. The EEG technician asks: “Doctor, would you like the recording displayed in referential or bipolar montage?” The attending says, “Show me the banana.” You have no idea what fruit has to do with neurology. But you need to learn — because the same brain activity can look completely different depending on how you display it. And one of those displays makes a seizure source obvious. The other can hide it.

You’ve learned where the electrodes go (the 10-20 system) and what the frequency bands mean. But there’s a piece missing between “recording the signal” and “reading the EEG”: the montage. This is how EEG signals get wired up for display — and it changes everything about what you see.

What Is a Montage?

The 10-20 system places electrodes in fixed positions on the scalp — that part doesn’t change. A montage is the math that happens after recording: how each displayed channel is built from the raw electrode voltages.

Here’s the fundamental principle: EEG measures voltage differences. A single electrode carries no meaningful voltage on its own — it needs a second point to compare against. Every channel on an EEG display is always a difference:

\[V_{\text{channel}} = V_{\text{electrode A}} - V_{\text{electrode B}}\]

The montage defines which electrode is A and which is B. Change the pairing, and the same brain activity produces a completely different-looking trace.

Analogy: Measuring elevation. You can express the height of a mountain relative to sea level — one fixed reference for everything — that’s a referential montage. Or as the slope between two neighboring points along a trail — that’s a bipolar montage. Same mountain, different description.

The Five Montages

Clinical EEG is displayed in one of five standard montages. Each is a different linear mapping from the 19 raw electrode voltages to the channels you see on screen:

Montage How each channel is built What it’s good at
Longitudinal bipolar (double banana) Electrode − neighbor along a front-to-back chain Localizing focal activity via phase reversal — the clinical default
Transverse bipolar Electrode − neighbor along a left-to-right chain Catching lateralized or inter-hemispheric asymmetries
Referential (Cz) Every electrode − Cz Amplitude mapping across the scalp (but Cz itself is always 0)
Average reference Every electrode − mean of all “Unbiased” view; standard in quantitative EEG / research
Laplacian Electrode − mean of its 4 spatial neighbors Sharpening focal sources; suppressing diffuse activity

You’ll meet all five in the interactive at the end of this module. But first, let’s drill into the clinical default — the longitudinal bipolar, nicknamed the double banana — because it’s what every neurologist defaults to when they sit down to read a study.

The Double Banana Montage

In a bipolar montage, each channel shows the voltage difference between two neighboring electrodes along a chain. The double banana has four chains running from front to back:

Chain Electrodes Derivations
Left parasagittal Fp1 → F3 → C3 → P3 → O1 Fp1-F3, F3-C3, C3-P3, P3-O1
Right parasagittal Fp2 → F4 → C4 → P4 → O2 Fp2-F4, F4-C4, C4-P4, P4-O2
Left temporal Fp1 → F7 → T3 → T5 → O1 Fp1-F7, F7-T3, T3-T5, T5-O1
Right temporal Fp2 → F8 → T4 → T6 → O2 Fp2-F8, F8-T4, T4-T6, T6-O2

The name comes from the shape of the chains — two curved lines running front-to-back on each side, like bananas. (Neurologists did not name this.)

Why is this montage the clinical default? Because EEG pathology — seizures, focal slowing, spikes — usually has a location. The front-to-back chains let you track where activity is strongest along the anterior-posterior axis. If a spike appears in F3-C3 and C3-P3 but not in Fp1-F3 or P3-O1, you know it’s centered around C3. If the left temporal chain lights up but the right doesn’t, it’s lateralized to the left.

Think about it: If each chain runs from front to back, what kind of activity would the double banana miss or underestimate? (Hint: what about activity that’s purely lateral — differences between left and right hemispheres at the same anterior-posterior level?)

The Dipole: Why EEG Looks the Way It Does

Before we can understand phase reversal properly, we need to understand what EEG actually measures. Not just “brain activity” — specifically, the electrical dipoles created by cortical pyramidal neurons.

When a group of pyramidal neurons receives synaptic input, current flows along their long apical dendrites. This creates a tiny current dipole — a separation of positive and negative charge, like a miniature battery. One end of the neuron is a current sink (negative), the other is a current source (positive).

A single neuron’s dipole is far too small to detect at the scalp. But when millions of pyramidal neurons fire in synchrony — all oriented the same way, all producing dipoles at the same time — their fields sum up. That summed dipole is what the EEG electrode detects, after the signal has been smeared and attenuated by cerebrospinal fluid, skull, and scalp.

Two important consequences:

  1. EEG has limited spatial resolution. You’re seeing the summed activity of millions of neurons, blurred through centimeters of tissue. You can’t pinpoint activity to a few millimeters — that requires intracranial electrodes.

  2. Dipole orientation matters. Pyramidal neurons on the crown of a gyrus produce radial dipoles that point straight toward the scalp — these are well-detected by nearby electrodes. But neurons on the wall of a sulcus produce tangential dipoles that point sideways — these produce a weaker, more spread-out signal, or may be nearly invisible to surface EEG. About two-thirds of the cortical surface is buried in sulci. A lot of brain activity never makes it to the scalp.

Why this matters clinically: Not all seizures show up on scalp EEG. A seizure originating deep in a sulcus might produce tangential dipoles that are invisible to surface electrodes. This is one reason why some epilepsy patients need intracranial EEG — electrodes placed directly on or in the brain — to find the seizure focus.

Phase Reversal: Localizing the Source

Clinical convention — negative is up. Real clinical EEG displays voltage with negative values pointing upward. This is the opposite of a normal math plot. It’s arbitrary (a 1950s convention), but universal in neurology. The widgets below follow this convention, so “up” means “more negative voltage” — the direction flips your signal-processing intuition by 180°.

Now the payoff. In a bipolar montage, when there’s a focal electrical source (a spike, a seizure onset), the two channels that share the electrode closest to the source will deflect in opposite directions. This is a phase reversal.

Why does this happen? Think about the math. Say electrode C3 has the largest negative voltage (it’s closest to the source). In the channel F3-C3 you compute (F3 − C3): F3 is less-negative than C3, so F3 − C3 is a positive number. On an EEG display (negative-up), positive values deflect down, so F3-C3 deflects down. In the next channel, C3-P3, you compute (C3 − P3): C3 very-negative minus P3 less-negative gives a large negative number — which deflects up.

Down in one channel, up in the next. The deflection reverses at C3. That’s the phase reversal, and it points you straight at the source.

Remember Module 1? You shifted two waves by 180 degrees and watched them become perfectly out of phase — when one went up, the other went down. That’s exactly what happens at a phase reversal. Same concept, now with real clinical stakes.

Try it yourself. The widget below simulates a bipolar montage with a movable spike source. Select where the spike originates, and watch the phase reversal follow it.

Phase reversal at C3
The spike source is at C3. Notice: Fp1-F3 shows little deflection, F3-C3 deflects DOWN, C3-P3 deflects UP, and P3-O1 shows little deflection. The direction reversal between F3-C3 and C3-P3 is the phase reversal — it tells you C3 is the source.

Experiment:

  • Start with the source at C3 (default). See the phase reversal between F3-C3 and C3-P3? One deflects down, the other deflects up — on a clinical EEG display, “up” means more negative voltage.
  • Move the source to P3. The phase reversal shifts — now it’s between C3-P3 and P3-O1.
  • Try F3 — the phase reversal is between Fp1-F3 and F3-C3.
  • Now try Fp1 or O1 — what happens? The source is at the end of the chain, so there’s only one neighboring channel. You get a deflection, but no full reversal. This is a real clinical limitation.

Phase reversal summary: In a bipolar montage, find the two adjacent channels where the deflection flips direction. The electrode they share is closest to the source. This is how neurologists localize spikes and seizure onsets on an EEG — and it’s the most important pattern recognition skill for reading a bipolar montage.

The montage is a lens, not the signal. The scalp topomap is the ground truth — the actual voltage at every point on the scalp. Every montage you can display is a linear combination of those 19 values. That’s why source localization algorithms work on the raw, un-montaged data — any single montage throws information away or reshapes it for the human eye.

Summary

You now understand the layer between raw electrode recordings and what appears on the screen:

  1. A montage defines how signals are displayed, not where electrodes go. It’s the pairing of electrodes into channels.
  2. The double banana montage is the clinical workhorse — four front-to-back chains of bipolar derivations, best at anterior-posterior localization.
  3. Cortical dipoles are the source of EEG signals. Radial dipoles (on gyrus crowns) are well-detected; tangential dipoles (in sulci) may be invisible. This limits what surface EEG can see.
  4. Phase reversal is the key localization tool in bipolar montages — the electrode where the deflection flips direction is closest to the source.

Next up: not everything on that EEG screen is brain activity. Eye blinks, muscle tension, heartbeats, and loose electrodes all contaminate the recording. Time to learn the difference.
