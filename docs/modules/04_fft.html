Module 4: FFT

What Frequencies Are Hiding in Your Signal?

NoteClinical scenario

Recording is clean now. But looking at the trace, you see only wiggles. Is the posterior alpha rhythm present? Is there abnormal slowing? Is there epileptiform activity in the theta or beta range? This module teaches you how the FFT lets you see what’s inside any EEG — the decomposition that makes diagnosis possible.

Your signal is clean (thanks to filtering). But looking at a wiggly line in the time domain is like listening to an orchestra and trying to identify every instrument by ear. The Fourier Transform is like a prism — it splits the signal into its individual frequency components so you can see exactly what’s inside.

This module teaches you the single most important analysis tool in signal processing: the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). By the end, you’ll be able to look at any signal and answer: what frequencies are in there, and how strong are they?

The Core Idea

Any signal — no matter how messy it looks — is just a sum of simple sine waves at different frequencies. The FFT decomposes a signal into those individual components.

Think of it this way:

  • Time domain: you see the signal as voltage changing over time (the wiggly line)
  • Frequency domain: you see which frequencies are present and how strong each one is (a bar chart of “ingredients”)

The FFT converts from the first view to the second. That’s it.

Frequency Resolution: Why Recording Duration Matters

Here’s a subtlety that matters in practice. Suppose your signal contains two frequencies that are close together — say 9 Hz and 11 Hz. Can the FFT tell them apart?

It depends on how long you record. The frequency resolution of the FFT is:

\[\Delta f = \frac{1}{T}\]

where \(T\) is the recording duration in seconds. A 1-second recording can resolve frequencies 1 Hz apart. A 0.5-second recording? Only 2 Hz apart — and those two peaks will blur into one.

Drag the slider and watch the two peaks separate (or merge).


Frequency resolution: 1.00 Hz ($\Delta f = 1/T$)

Frequency resolution = \(1/T\), where \(T\) is the recording duration. Want to distinguish frequencies 1 Hz apart? You need at least 1 second of data. Want 0.5 Hz resolution? Record for 2 seconds. This is a fundamental tradeoff: better frequency resolution requires longer recordings.

This matters clinically. A 1-second EEG epoch can tell you whether activity is roughly “alpha-range” or “beta-range.” But to precisely characterize a 9 Hz vs. 11 Hz rhythm, you need several seconds of clean data.

Experiment:

  • Set duration to 0.5 seconds. The two peaks are invisible — just one merged bump.
  • Slowly increase to 1 second. Can you start to see a hint of separation?
  • At 2-3 seconds, the two peaks are clearly distinct.
  • At 5 seconds, they’re razor-sharp. More data = more precision.

Why This Matters Clinically

The FFT is behind every frequency analysis in clinical neurophysiology:

  • EEG interpretation: identifying dominant rhythms (alpha, beta, theta, delta)
  • Sleep staging: each sleep stage has a characteristic power spectrum
  • Seizure detection: abnormal rhythmic activity shows up as unexpected spectral peaks
  • Monitoring: brain-computer interfaces and neurofeedback systems use real-time FFT
  • Artifact detection: 60 Hz peaks = power line noise; broad spectral bumps = muscle artifact

Every time a monitor displays a “power spectrum” or “spectral analysis” — whether it’s in the EEG lab, the OR, or the ICU — it’s running an FFT under the hood.

Summary

You can now decompose any signal into its frequency components. The FFT takes a confusing time-domain signal and reveals its “ingredients” — which frequencies are present and how strong each one is.

Three things to remember:

  1. The FFT is a prism — it splits a mixed signal into individual frequency components
  2. Peaks in the spectrum = frequencies in the signal — clean peaks mean periodic activity; a sharp 60 Hz peak means noise
  3. Resolution = \(1/T\) — longer recordings give you finer frequency detail

But so far we’ve been working with clean synthetic signals — sums of perfect sine waves. Real brain signals are messier, more interesting, and carry clinical meaning. Time to meet actual EEG.
