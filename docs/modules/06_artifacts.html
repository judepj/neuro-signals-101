Module 6: Artifacts

Brain or Noise? — Telling Real EEG from Contamination

A medical student is shadowing in the epilepsy monitoring unit. The patient’s EEG suddenly shows a massive, sharp deflection. The student’s eyes go wide: “Is that a seizure?!” The attending neurologist glances at the screen, then at the patient, and says flatly: “No. She blinked.” Knowing the difference between an artifact and a seizure could change a diagnosis — or prevent an unnecessary one.

You can now read an EEG — electrode positions, frequency bands, basic patterns. But here’s the dirty secret: a lot of what shows up on the EEG screen isn’t brain activity at all. Eye blinks, muscle tension, heartbeats, loose electrodes, power lines — they all contaminate the recording. An experienced neurologist can spot these instantly. By the end of this module, so can you.

The Five Usual Suspects

Every EEG reader learns to recognize these five artifacts on sight:

Artifact Source Key Feature Where It’s Worst
Eye blink Corneoretinal dipole Large, slow, positive deflection Fp1, Fp2 (frontal)
Muscle (EMG) Jaw clenching, frowning High-frequency “fuzz” Temporal electrodes
60 Hz line noise Power lines Perfect sinusoidal ripple All channels equally
ECG Heart’s electrical field Periodic sharp waves at heart rate Diffuse, ~1 Hz
Electrode pop Loose or faulty electrode Sudden spike, single channel Just one electrode

Let’s learn to recognize each one — and then practice.

Tips for the game:

  • Look for sudden, large deflections that don’t match the ongoing background — could be a blink or electrode pop.
  • Listen for high-frequency fuzz — that’s muscle.
  • Look for perfect regularity — 60 Hz noise or ECG.
  • Clean EEG has a natural, somewhat irregular rhythm. Artifacts tend to stand out because they don’t “fit” the background.

Artifact rejection is not optional. If you analyze contaminated EEG, your results are wrong. Garbage in, garbage out. The three main strategies are:

  1. Prevention — Good electrode application, low impedances, relaxed patient
  2. Rejection — Identify and discard contaminated epochs
  3. Correction — Use signal processing (notch filters, ICA) to remove the artifact while keeping the brain signal

In clinical practice, you’ll use all three. Prevention is always best.

Why Artifacts Fool People

Here’s the thing that makes artifacts dangerous: some artifacts look like real brain pathology.

  • An eye blink can look like a frontal spike (which could mean epilepsy)
  • A muscle artifact can look like high-frequency beta activity (which has clinical significance)
  • An ECG artifact can look like periodic discharges (which can indicate serious brain injury)

The difference is in the distribution (which channels are affected), the morphology (exact shape), and the context (what the patient was doing). A skilled EEG reader checks all three before making a call.

The artifact checklist — Before calling something abnormal, ask:

  1. Is it in only one electrode? Probably electrode pop or bad contact.
  2. Is it only in frontal channels? Could be an eye blink.
  3. Is it perfectly periodic? Check if it matches the heart rate (ECG) or a power-line harmonic (60/120 Hz).
  4. Is it broadband and “fuzzy”? Likely muscle.
  5. Does it correlate with patient behavior? Ask the tech: “Was the patient doing anything when this happened?”

What’s Next

You can now tell brain from noise. With clean EEG in hand, there’s one more power tool to learn: instead of looking at frequencies averaged over the whole recording, what if you could watch them change in real time? That’s the spectrogram — and it’s how neurologists track brain dynamics as they unfold.
