Module 7: Spectrograms

Watching the Brain Change — Frequency Over Time

A patient in the epilepsy monitoring unit has a seizure. The neurologist pulls up the spectrogram on the review station: a sudden burst of color sweeps across the display — low frequencies first, then evolving to faster rhythmic activity, then abruptly stopping. In one image, you can see the seizure’s entire story: onset, evolution, and termination. No other single view gives you this much information this fast.

So far you’ve used the FFT to look at which frequencies are present in a signal — averaged over the whole recording. But the brain doesn’t stay in one state. A patient falls asleep, wakes up, has a seizure, recovers. The frequencies change over time. A spectrogram shows you when each frequency appears — it’s like a movie of the brain’s frequency content.

The Idea

An FFT tells you what frequencies are present. A spectrogram tells you what frequencies are present and when.

The recipe is straightforward:

  1. Chop the signal into short, overlapping time windows
  2. Compute the FFT of each window
  3. Stack the results side by side: time on the x-axis, frequency on the y-axis, color = power

That’s it. Each vertical slice of the spectrogram is just an FFT of a short segment. String them together, and you can watch frequencies come and go.

A spectrogram is a stack of FFTs. Each column is the frequency content of one short time window. Together they show you how the brain’s frequency mix changes over time — sleep stages, seizure evolution, drug effects, and more.

The Window Size Tradeoff

Here’s the catch with spectrograms. There’s a fundamental tradeoff — almost like a Heisenberg uncertainty principle for time-frequency analysis:

You can’t have perfect time resolution and perfect frequency resolution at the same time.

  • Small window = good time resolution (you know when something changed) but poor frequency resolution (frequencies are blurry)
  • Large window = good frequency resolution (clean, sharp frequency bands) but poor time resolution (changes are smeared over time)

The math is simple: if your window is \(T\) seconds long, your frequency resolution is \(\Delta f = 1/T\) Hz. A 1-second window gives you 1 Hz resolution. A 0.25-second window gives you 4 Hz resolution — you can’t distinguish frequencies closer than 4 Hz apart.

The time-frequency tradeoff:

  • Frequency resolution = 1 / window size
  • Time resolution = window size
  • You cannot improve both simultaneously. Every spectrogram is a compromise.

In clinical EEG, a window of 0.5–2 seconds is typical. For seizure detection, you want to see fast changes (shorter window). For sleep staging, you can afford longer windows (better frequency resolution).

Clinical Applications

Spectrograms are used daily in clinical neuroscience:

Application What You See Window Size
Seizure detection Evolving frequency pattern: onset → spread → termination 0.5–1s
Sleep staging Slow progression through frequency bands (awake→NREM→REM) 2–5s
Anesthesia monitoring Frequency shift as drugs take effect 1–2s
Brain-computer interfaces Motor imagery → frequency changes in mu/beta bands 0.25–0.5s
ICU monitoring Long-term trends: detecting burst suppression or status epilepticus 1–5s

In each case, the spectrogram gives clinicians a rapid visual summary that would take much longer to extract from raw EEG traces.
