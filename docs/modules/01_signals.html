Module 1: Signals

What Electrical Signals from the Body Look Like

A 28-year-old woman comes to your clinic with episodes of “staring off” lasting 10–15 seconds, happening several times a day. Her partner says she doesn’t respond during these episodes. You order an EEG. The technician hands you a tracing. It looks like this:

Simulated EEG showing rhythmic 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharges — the hallmark of an absence seizure.

Can you tell what’s happening? When does the abnormal activity start? What’s different about it?

By the end of this tutorial, you’ll be able to answer those questions — and understand why the signal looks the way it does. But first, we need to learn the language that describes any electrical signal from the body.

The Basics

Every cell in your body produces tiny electrical signals. Neurons fire. Heart muscle contracts. Skeletal muscle twitches. These are not abstract physics — these are your patient’s physiology, translated into voltage over time.

A signal is just a measurement that changes over time. When we record an EEG, ECG, or EMG, we’re measuring voltage (in microvolts or millivolts) at each moment.

The simplest signal you can imagine is a smooth, repeating wave. Three properties describe it completely: amplitude — how tall the wave is, which tells you how strong the signal is; frequency — how fast it repeats, in cycles per second; and phase — where in its cycle the wave sits at any given moment. Three knobs. Let’s play with them.

Phase Across Electrodes

You’ve already played with phase in the signal builder above. Here’s why neurologists care: when two nearby electrodes record signals that are out of phase with each other, that difference is often the clue that tells you where a seizure is starting.

Drag the slider below. Both waves have the same frequency and amplitude — only the phase differs, as if two electrodes were sitting on opposite sides of the same generator.


Both waves are identical. Try shifting the purple wave by 180° — now they're perfectly out of sync.

This matters clinically. Neurologists use phase reversal — where one electrode’s signal flips relative to its neighbor — to pinpoint exactly where a seizure starts. Same concept as the slider above, real clinical stakes.

Why Does This Matter Clinically?

Different brain states produce different frequencies and amplitudes:

Brain State Dominant Frequency Typical Amplitude What’s Happening
Deep sleep 1–4 Hz (delta) 100–200 μV Large populations of neurons firing in sync
Relaxed, eyes closed 8–13 Hz (alpha) 30–50 μV Thalamo-cortical idle rhythm
Focused attention 13–30 Hz (beta) 10–20 μV Cortical neurons busy with different tasks
Active thinking >30 Hz (gamma) 5–10 μV Local, fast processing

Notice the pattern: slower rhythms tend to be bigger, faster rhythms tend to be smaller. This isn’t a coincidence — slow rhythms reflect more neurons firing together (synchrony), which sums to a larger voltage at the scalp.

Amplitude tells you how much electrical activity is reaching the electrode, and frequency tells you how fast that activity is oscillating. Together they are the first two things you assess when you look at any physiological recording — EEG, ECG, or EMG.

It’s Not Just the Brain

The same two properties — amplitude and frequency — describe signals from other parts of the body:

Signal Typical Amplitude Typical Frequency Source
EEG (brain) 10–100 μV 1–100 Hz Cortical pyramidal neurons
ECG (heart) 0.5–3 mV ~1 Hz (60 bpm) Cardiac muscle depolarization
EMG (muscle) 0.1–5 mV 20–500 Hz Motor unit action potentials

The ECG is about 1,000× larger than the EEG. The EMG has much higher frequencies than either. These differences aren’t trivia — they determine what equipment you need, what filters you set, and what artifacts you worry about. (More on artifacts in Module 6.)

Summary

You now know three things about any physiological signal: amplitude (how tall the wave is — μV for EEG, mV for ECG and EMG), frequency (how many cycles per second, in Hz), and phase (where in the cycle it sits at any moment). You’ve also seen that real signals aren’t single pure waves — they’re sums of many components layered together, each with its own amplitude, frequency, and phase. That last idea is what makes the Fourier transform (Module 4) so useful: it takes a messy recorded signal and pulls the components back apart.
