Module 3: Filtering

Cleaning the Signal — Separating Brain Waves from Noise

NoteClinical scenario

The EEG is back. You pull it up. Baseline’s drifting, 60 Hz mains noise is everywhere, the prep room was next to the MRI control panel. “Can we salvage this?” you ask the tech. This module teaches you what filters do — and what they can hide.

Your EEG recording is full of stuff you don’t want. 60 Hz power line noise. Slow electrode drift (the baseline wanders up and down over seconds). Muscle artifact from the patient clenching their jaw. Filtering is how you separate what matters from what doesn’t — like adjusting the bass and treble on a stereo, but for brain waves.

The idea is simple: every signal is a mixture of different frequencies. A filter lets certain frequencies through and blocks others. The question is always: which frequencies do you keep?

The Anatomy of a Dirty Signal

Before we filter anything, let’s see what we’re dealing with. The signal below is synthetic, but it’s a realistic mix of what you’d see in a real EEG recording:

  • A slow drift (0.3 Hz) (baseline wander from electrode impedance changes)
  • A 10 Hz alpha rhythm (the brain signal you actually want)
  • A 60 Hz line noise (power line interference)
  • Some random noise (thermal noise from electronics)

Showing: Raw signal — all components present.

Experiment:

  • Click Raw — this is what the technician sees before any filtering. Hard to read, right?
  • Click High-pass (1 Hz) — the slow drift disappears, but the 60 Hz fuzz is still there.
  • Click Low-pass (40 Hz) — the 60 Hz noise vanishes, but now the slow drift is back.
  • Click Band-pass (1–40 Hz) — both the drift and line noise are gone. This is the standard clinical EEG filter. The 10 Hz alpha wave is clearly visible.

The three basic filter types:

  • High-pass filter: Removes frequencies below the cutoff. Lets high frequencies through. Removes slow drift.
  • Low-pass filter: Removes frequencies above the cutoff. Lets low frequencies through. Removes fast noise.
  • Band-pass filter: Combination of high-pass and low-pass. Only lets frequencies between two cutoffs through. This is what you’ll use most in clinical EEG.

The standard clinical EEG display filter is 1–40 Hz band-pass (sometimes 0.5–70 Hz for more detail).

Interactive: Adjustable Band-Pass Filter

Now you control the filter cutoffs. Drag the sliders and watch the signal change in real time. The small plot on the right shows the filter’s frequency response — which frequencies it lets through (green) and which it blocks (red).



Experiment:

  • Set the low cutoff to 1 Hz and high cutoff to 40 Hz. This is the standard clinical EEG filter. The signal should look clean.
  • Now lower the high cutoff to 15 Hz. You’ll lose the beta rhythm. Lower it to 8 Hz — now even alpha disappears. You’re over-filtering.
  • Set the high cutoff back to 40 Hz and raise the low cutoff to 8 Hz. Now you’re only seeing alpha and above — the theta and delta rhythms are gone. Good for highlighting alpha, bad if you need to see sleep slow waves.
  • Watch the frequency response plot on the right. The green circles are components that survive the filter; the red Xs are blocked.

The Notch Filter: Surgical Noise Removal

Sometimes you don’t want to block a range of frequencies — you want to remove a single, specific frequency. The classic example: 60 Hz power line noise (50 Hz in Europe). A notch filter cuts out a narrow band around one frequency and leaves everything else untouched.

The widget below shows a signal with clear 10 Hz brain activity contaminated by 60 Hz line noise. Toggle the notch filter and watch it disappear — both in the time domain (left) and the frequency domain (right).

60 Hz line noise is contaminating the signal.

Watch carefully:

  • With the notch OFF, look at the time domain signal — the fast 60 Hz oscillation rides on top of the slower alpha wave, making it look “hairy.”
  • Toggle the notch ON. The signal instantly smooths out, and in the power spectrum, the 60 Hz peak disappears while the 10 Hz peak stays perfectly intact.
  • Toggle back and forth a few times. This is exactly what happens in the clinic when you apply a notch filter.

The notch filter tradeoff: A notch filter at 60 Hz removes power line noise beautifully. But it also removes any brain activity that happens to be at 60 Hz — specifically, gamma oscillations, which are involved in attention, perception, and binding of sensory information. In most clinical settings, this is an acceptable tradeoff (gamma is hard to see on scalp EEG anyway). But in research settings where gamma activity matters, alternatives like shielded rooms and careful grounding are used instead.

Filter Rules of Thumb for Clinical EEG

Here’s what you’ll see on most EEG machines:

Setting Typical Value What It Does
High-pass filter 0.5–1 Hz Removes electrode drift and DC offset
Low-pass filter 35–70 Hz Removes muscle artifact and high-frequency noise
Notch filter 60 Hz (US) / 50 Hz (EU) Removes power line interference
Standard display 1–40 Hz band-pass The “default view” — clean enough for most clinical reads

These are display filters — they change what you see, not what’s stored. Modern digital EEG systems record the raw data and let you adjust the filters afterward. This is one of the great advantages of digital over analog: you can always go back and re-filter.

Why Would a Doctor Care About This?

Because filter settings change what you see, and what you see changes your diagnosis.

  • Too aggressive high-pass (e.g., 5 Hz): You’ll miss slow waves — including the hallmark of absence seizures (3 Hz spike-and-wave from Module 1) if they have slow components.
  • Too aggressive low-pass (e.g., 15 Hz): You’ll miss fast activity — including spikes, which have high-frequency content.
  • No notch filter: The 60 Hz noise may obscure subtle seizure patterns.
  • Notch filter always on: You might miss gamma-frequency abnormalities (rare, but it happens).

The attending doesn’t just “read” an EEG — they adjust the filters, re-read, adjust again. It’s an interactive process.

Quiz: Check Your Understanding

Quiz

Q1: An EEG recording shows slow undulations with a period of about 5 seconds (0.2 Hz). Which filter type removes this artifact?

Q2: You apply a band-pass filter of 1–40 Hz to an EEG recording. A 50 Hz muscle artifact is present. Does it survive the filter?

Q3: A notch filter at 60 Hz removes power line noise. Could it also remove brain activity at 60 Hz?

Summary

Filtering is your first line of defense against noise. The key ideas:

  1. High-pass filters remove slow drift (low frequencies). Standard EEG: 0.5–1 Hz cutoff.
  2. Low-pass filters remove fast noise and muscle artifact (high frequencies). Standard EEG: 35–70 Hz cutoff.
  3. Band-pass filters combine both — they define the window of frequencies you care about.
  4. Notch filters surgically remove a single frequency (typically 60 Hz power line noise).
  5. Every filter is a tradeoff — removing noise always risks removing signal. Clinical judgment determines the right balance.

The signal is clean. But right now we’re looking at wiggly lines in the time domain. What if we could peek inside and see exactly which frequencies are present, and how strong each one is? That’s the Fourier Transform — and it’s the single most important tool in signal processing.
